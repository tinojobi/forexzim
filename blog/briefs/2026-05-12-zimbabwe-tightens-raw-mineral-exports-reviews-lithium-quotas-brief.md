# Research Brief: Zimbabwe Tightens Grip on Raw Mineral Exports, Reviews Lithium Quotas

STORY: Zimbabwe Tightens Grip on Raw Mineral Exports, Reviews Lithium Quotas
CLUSTER_ID: zimbabwe-lithium-export-quotas-review-may-2026

## KEY FACTS (with confidence tags):

- [HIGH] Zimbabwe suspended exports of all raw minerals and lithium concentrates on February 26, 2026 (Source: Reuters + Zimbabwe Mail + NewsDay + Africanews)
- [HIGH] Ban was fast-tracked from original Jan 2027 date due to "unacceptable scramble" by miners rushing to export (Source: Africanews + Zimbabwe Mail)
- [HIGH] Government alleged malpractices: under-invoicing, revenue leakages, illicit stockpiling in neighbouring countries (Source: Reuters + Zimbabwe Mail)
- [HIGH] Mines Ministry letter (April 2) to Chamber of Mines set conditions: financial statement publication, labour/environmental compliance, lithium sulphate plant commitments by Jan 1 2027, individual quotas, 10% export tax (Source: Reuters + Africa Business Insight + MiningMX)
- [HIGH] Six companies authorized: Sinomine (Bikita), Chengxin (Sabi Star), Yahua (Kamativi), Huayou (Arcadia), Tsingshan (Gwanda), Kuvimba (Sandawana) (Source: Ecofin Agency)
- [HIGH] Minister Kambamura (May 13): quotas are a "monitoring mechanism"; will be reviewed in 2027 based on processing capacity (Source: New Zimbabwe)
- [HIGH] 2025 exports: 1.128 million metric tonnes spodumene concentrate to China = ~15% of China's imports (Source: Reuters + Africanews + multiple)
- [HIGH] 2025 revenue: US$571.6 million (Source: MMCZ via Africanews)
- [HIGH] Huayou built $400 million lithium sulphate plant at Arcadia (Source: Reuters + multiple)
- [HIGH] Sinomine announced $500 million lithium sulphate plant at Bikita (Source: Reuters)
- [HIGH] 10% export tax on concentrates continues until Jan 2027 full ban (Source: Reuters + multiple)
- [MEDIUM] Huayou reportedly had NOT received quota allocation (Source: Zimbabwe Mail only)
- [MEDIUM] Analysts call this "pragmatic adjustment, not full policy reversal" (Source: NewsDay)
- [HIGH] Chengxin annual output ~290,000 mt covered by quota; Sinomine got 200,000 mt (Source: Zimbabwe Mail)
- [HIGH] Mining ~80% of exports, ~19% of government revenue (Source: Africa Business Insight)
- [HIGH] Deputy Mines Minister Fred Moyo: allocations vary by producer capacity and compliance (Source: Zimbabwe Mail)
- [HIGH] Additional conditions: two internationally accredited labs within 3 months, on-site assay labs, SHE departments, monthly progress reports (Source: Hurumende)
- [HIGH] Sandawana Mine: $275 million concentrator plant planned, commissioning Dec 2027 (Source: Zimbabwe Mail via Chiwenga tour)

## KEY QUOTES:

- "We also put those quotas as a monitoring mechanism, whereby we monitor progress with regard to conditions that were given by the government. Are they implementing those conditions, which include, but not limited to, setting up separating facilities of other economic materials or minerals within the lithium concentrates" — Polite Kambamura, Mines and Mining Development Minister (New Zimbabwe, May 13)

- "Instead of preparing for value addition, some actors engaged in a frenzy of mining activity, seeking to extract and export as much raw lithium as possible before the deadline" — Ndavaningi Mangwana, Information Secretary (Africanews, March 4)

## CONTEXT:

- China dominates Zimbabwe lithium: Huayou, Sinomine, Chengxin, Yahua, Tsingshan
- Global lithium oversupply since 2023 has depressed prices; rebounding above $2,000/t in 2026
- Zimbabwe is Africa's largest lithium producer
- Feb ban caused immediate forex disruption; quota system partially restores flows
- Energy grid constraints (Kariba Dam load shedding) challenge processing ambitions
- Vision 2030: shift from raw exports to value-added manufacturing
