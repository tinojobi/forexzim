# Research Brief: Zimbabwe Counterfeit Goods Law

## KEY FACTS
- [HIGH] Zimbabwe's Government is considering a dedicated Counterfeit Act to address counterfeit and substandard goods in shops and informal markets. Sources: iHarare, Zimbabwe Situation/Herald.
- [HIGH] Industry and Commerce Minister Mangaliso Ndlovu said counterfeit products threaten competitiveness, revenue inflows and consumer health. Sources: iHarare, Zimbabwe Situation/Herald.
- [HIGH] Authorities reported more than 5,087 businesses inspected, over 560 prosecutions finalised, 589 compliance notices issued and 6,189 seizures effected. Sources: iHarare, Zimbabwe Situation/Herald.
- [HIGH] A Ministry of Industry and Commerce survey with the Standards Association of Zimbabwe found about 50% of sampled products from informal retail shelves failed required standards. Sources: NewZimbabwe, ZimLive.
- [HIGH] Examples cited by officials include fake Vaseline, flour, rice and toothpaste. Sources: NewZimbabwe, ZimLive, iHarare.
- [MEDIUM] The Consumer Protection Policy 2026 to 2030 includes anti-counterfeiting measures targeted for 2027 and stronger consumer protection institutions. Source: Zimbabwe Situation/Herald.
- [MEDIUM] Industry stakeholders link the issue to Zimbabwe's AfCFTA participation and the need for quality safeguards around a market valued at roughly US$2.3 trillion. Sources: iHarare, Zimbabwe Situation/Herald.

## KEY QUOTES
- Mangaliso Ndlovu: counterfeit products threaten the national economy by reducing competitiveness, compromising revenue inflows and exposing consumers to health risks.
- Douglas Runyowa: 50 percent of tested products did not comply, including fake Vaseline, flour, rice and toothpaste.
- Munyaradzi Hwengwere: Zimbabwe should use AfCFTA to access a US$2.3 trillion market, not become poorer because of it.

## CONTEXT NEEDED FOR READER
- This is not only a consumer protection story. It affects formal retailers, local manufacturers, tax revenue and Zimbabwe's ability to compete under regional trade rules.
- Informal markets often win on price, but weak standards enforcement can shift hidden costs to consumers and formal businesses.
