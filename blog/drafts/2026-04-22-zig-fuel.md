Zimbabwe rolled out shiny new ZiG banknotes on April 7. The "Big Five" notes, as the RBZ calls them, are supposed to be tougher, harder to fake, and longer-lasting. But there is one place you still cannot spend them: the fuel pump.

Arthur Mutsvene, a Harare resident, put it bluntly in a [street interview with New Zimbabwe](https://www.newzimbabwe.com/even-if-you-introduce-zig500-its-useless-citizens-react-to-rollout-of-new-banknotes/): "These bank notes are not allowed at any fuel pump while that is our own Zimbabwean currency, so why is it they cannot buy essentials?"

Fair question.

## The RBZ's mixed messages

RBZ Governor John Mushayavanhu has been all over the place on this. When ZiG launched in April 2024, he said fuel dealers were not required to sell in the local currency. Then, after parliament grilled him about it, he told a radio station that fuel companies would "eventually" be compelled to accept ZiG because they need to pay 50 percent of their taxes in the local currency ([New Zimbabwe](https://www.newzimbabwe.com/law-will-catch-up-with-fuel-dealers-not-accepting-zig-says-rbz-governor/)).

"If the fuel dealer is not selling the fuel in ZiG, they will find themselves in situations where they are looking for ZiG come June-July," he warned.

But "eventually" is doing a lot of heavy lifting there. As of today, fuel remains priced in USD at every pump I am aware of. The governor told legislators that pricing negotiations with fuel suppliers are ongoing, and that "the outcome will be known to the public soon" ([New Zimbabwe](https://www.newzimbabwe.com/legislators-grill-rbz-governor-mushayavanhu-over-zigs-failure-to-purchase-fuel/)). That was a while ago.

## Why this matters so much

Fuel is not just another commodity. It is the thing everyone buys. Transport costs, food prices, electricity generation, they all run through fuel. If you cannot use your own currency to buy fuel, what confidence are you supposed to have in that currency?

Right now, [85 percent of transactions in Zimbabwe happen in USD](https://www.newzimbabwe.com/legislators-grill-rbz-governor-mushayavanhu-over-zigs-failure-to-purchase-fuel/), with only 15 percent in ZiG. That gap is not closing. The new banknotes were supposed to help. Better quality, more durable, harder to counterfeit. All good things. But notes people cannot spend are just paper with extra steps.

The parallel market is also not helping. When the official rate was around 13.6 ZiG to 1 USD, street rates were hitting 20 to 1, according to parliamentary testimony. ## Outside Harare, it is worse

In Harare, you can at least find shops and vendors who accept ZiG for some goods. But in provinces like Masvingo and Matabeleland, people rely on the South African rand and US dollar. ZiG usage is mostly concentrated in the capital. If you cannot buy fuel with ZiG in Harare, you definitely cannot buy it in Bulawayo or Masvingo.

## What needs to happen

The governor keeps saying fuel dealers will eventually have to accept ZiG because of tax obligations. That is a reasonable argument on paper. But the timeline keeps slipping, and every month that passes without fuel available in ZiG erodes whatever trust is left.

If the government wants ZiG to work, fuel acceptance is not optional. It is the single biggest test of whether this currency can function in the real economy. New notes with anti-counterfeit features do not matter if the most important commodity in the country is priced exclusively in someone else's money.
