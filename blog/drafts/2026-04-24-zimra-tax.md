If you make money online in Zimbabwe, sell tomatoes at Mupedzanhamo, rent out a house in Mbare, or drive for Bolt, ZIMRA just sent you a message. Come forward and pay up by June 30, 2026, or we come find you.

That is the gist of the [voluntary disclosure programme](https://www.newsday.co.zw/business/article/200054376/zimra-rolls-out-tax-amnesty-for-2025-targets-informal-sector-and-digital-earners) the Zimbabwe Revenue Authority rolled out on April 23. The pitch sounds friendly enough. Disclose your undeclared income for the 2025 tax year, and they waive the penalties. Interest still applies, but no audit and no prosecution. Sounds reasonable until you read the fine print about what happens after the deadline.

After June 30, ZIMRA says it will bring the full force of enforcement. That means penalties, audits, and possible criminal charges for anyone caught hiding income.

## Who is this really about?

The programme casts a wide net. ZIMRA specifically mentions informal trading, rental income, digital services, crypto transactions, and cross-border earnings. If that sounds like half the Zimbabwean economy, you would be right.

The informal sector makes up the bulk of economic activity in the country. Most of it runs on US dollars, cash in hand, no receipts, no paper trail. People sell forex at the parallel market. Freelancers get paid through PayPal or Wise. Cross-border traders move goods between South Africa, Zambia, and Mozambique without a single tax invoice.

All of that is now in ZIMRA's crosshairs.

## Content creators got special attention

The crackdown got personal when Madam Boss, one of Zimbabwe's biggest social media personalities, revealed she earns up to US$20,000 a month from Facebook ([iHarare](https://iharare.com/zimbabwe-creators-under-zimra-tax-spotlight-after-madam-boss-us20k-reveal/)). That number lit a fire under the tax authority.

ZIMRA now says digital income is "firmly on our radar" and that anyone earning from platforms like Facebook, YouTube, or TikTok needs to register, declare, and pay. Several other influencers including Mai TT, DJ Towers, and Ritz are also expected to comply.

Content creators were given an earlier deadline of May 30, 2026 to regularise their affairs.

## New taxes on top of the amnesty

This amnesty is not happening in a vacuum. Starting January 2026, eight new tax measures came into force ([iHarare](https://iharare.com/8-new-taxes-now-in-force-in-zimbabwe-from-january-2026-govt-confirms/)):

- A 15% Digital Services Withholding Tax on payments to foreign platforms like Netflix, Spotify, and Starlink
- VAT went up from 15% to 15.5%
- A 15% presumptive tax on rental income from commercial properties
- Gambling tax on winnings jumped from 10% to 25%

Opposition politician Tendai Biti called Zimbabwe the most taxed nation in Africa and warned that these new levies would cripple the economy further ([New Zimbabwe](https://www.newzimbabwe.com/zimbabwe-most-taxed-nation-in-africa-biti-warns-new-punitive-absurd-taxes-will-further-cripple-zimbabwes-economy/)). He is not wrong about the squeeze, but the government needs revenue and the informal sector is where the money hides.

## What this means for forex rates

Here is the part that matters for anyone watching the [ZimRate exchange rates](https://www.zimrate.com).

A huge chunk of Zimbabwe's forex market operates informally. Cross-border traders, forex dealers, and online freelancers all deal in USD outside the formal banking system. If ZIMRA successfully pulls more of these people into the tax net, it could push more transactions through formal channels.

More formal forex flows mean better data for the central bank, which affects how the official exchange rate is set. It also means some of that parallel market USD might dry up, which could widen the gap between the official and street rates.

## Should you come forward?

That depends on how exposed you are. If ZIMRA can see your money, through bank transfers, mobile money, or a Facebook page where you advertise your hustle, the risk of staying quiet is real. Penalties after the deadline could be brutal.

If you have been operating completely off the grid with cash only, the calculus is different. But ZIMRA is also pushing digitalisation hard, with plans to hit US$7.2 billion in revenue by 2030 ([Herald](https://www.heraldonline.co.zw/zimra-intensifies-digitalisation-to-reach-us72bn-revenue-milestone/)). The walls are closing in slowly.

The smart play for most people is to take the amnesty. Pay what you owe, keep your penalties, and move on with your life. The tax man always catches up eventually.
