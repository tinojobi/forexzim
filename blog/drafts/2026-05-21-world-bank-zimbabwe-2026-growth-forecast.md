<p>The World Bank has lifted Zimbabwe's 2026 growth forecast to 5%, giving the country a stronger outlook than its earlier 4.6% projection (<a href="https://www.newzimbabwe.com/world-bank-revises-zimbabwes-2026-growth-forecast-to-5/">NewZimbabwe</a>). The bank's January 2026 Global Economic Prospects table also puts Zimbabwe's 2025 growth estimate at 6.6% and keeps the 2027 forecast at 5.0% (<a href="https://thedocs.worldbank.org/en/doc/7ce50b5aa95bef66048680bba9926ec8-0050012026/related/GEP-Jan-2026-Regional-Highlights-SSA.pdf">World Bank</a>).</p>

<p>That is a notable shift for an economy that expanded by only 1.7% in 2024, according to the same World Bank table (<a href="https://thedocs.worldbank.org/en/doc/7ce50b5aa95bef66048680bba9926ec8-0050012026/related/GEP-Jan-2026-Regional-Highlights-SSA.pdf">World Bank</a>). The better number is not magic. It rests on two familiar engines: farms and mines.</p>

<h2>Agriculture and mining carry the upgrade</h2>

<p>In its Sub-Saharan Africa analysis, the World Bank said Zimbabwe's growth increased sharply in 2025 because agricultural production recovered and investment rose in extractive sectors such as gold, lithium, iron and steel (<a href="https://thedocs.worldbank.org/en/doc/7ce50b5aa95bef66048680bba9926ec8-0050012026/related/GEP-Jan-2026-Analysis-SSA.pdf">World Bank</a>). That mix matters because agriculture feeds rural incomes, while mining brings foreign currency into the formal economy.</p>

<p>The World Bank's Zimbabwe Economic Update also projects 2025 GDP growth at 6.6%, supported by agriculture, services, mining and steel investment (<a href="https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/feature/2025/12/02/zimbabwe-economic-update-2025-fostering-a-business-enabling-regulatory-environment-for-private-sector-growth">World Bank</a>). If those sectors keep moving, the 5% forecast for 2026 looks less like a one-off bounce and more like a test of whether recovery can survive normal political and weather pressure.</p>

<h2>The fiscal policy angle</h2>

<p>The forecast also lands at a sensitive budget moment. Zimbabwe's 2026 budget is built around expected revenue of about US$9.4 billion and planned expenditure of about US$9.5 billion, leaving a small projected deficit of US$105.9 million (<a href="https://www.newsday.co.zw/theindependent/local-news/article/200049064/growth-forecast-cut-to-5-the-nearly-balanced-us95bn-budget-counts-on-inflation-stability-above-normal-rainfall-to-keep-recovery-on-track">Zimbabwe Independent</a>). NewZimbabwe reported that the deficit target is around 0.2% of GDP (<a href="https://www.newzimbabwe.com/world-bank-revises-zimbabwes-2026-growth-forecast-to-5/">NewZimbabwe</a>).</p>

<p>A near-balanced budget is good for credibility, but it also narrows the room for expensive surprises. If agriculture underperforms, mineral prices fall, or exchange-rate pressure returns, fiscal policy will have to choose between protecting stability and cushioning households. That is easier said than done.</p>

<p>The World Bank has already flagged fiscal slippages, external shocks and climate-related disasters as risks to Zimbabwe's medium-term outlook (<a href="https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/feature/2025/12/02/zimbabwe-economic-update-2025-fostering-a-business-enabling-regulatory-environment-for-private-sector-growth">World Bank</a>). Those warnings are not abstract in Zimbabwe, where one bad rainfall season can quickly move food imports, rural incomes and budget assumptions in the wrong direction.</p>

<h2>Inflation and the ZiG test</h2>

<p>The brighter growth outlook is tied to the inflation story. The World Bank says tight monetary policy since late 2024 helped improve inflation dynamics and stabilise the ZiG, with inflation expected to moderate to single digits in 2026 and move toward 5% over the medium term (<a href="https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/feature/2025/12/02/zimbabwe-economic-update-2025-fostering-a-business-enabling-regulatory-environment-for-private-sector-growth">World Bank</a>).</p>

<p>That is where fiscal discipline becomes more than an accounting target. If spending pressure forces new arrears, money creation, or sudden tax changes, the inflation gains can fade quickly. For a country still rebuilding trust in the local currency, the 5% growth forecast is only as strong as the policy discipline behind it.</p>
