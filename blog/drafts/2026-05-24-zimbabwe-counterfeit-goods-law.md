<p>Zimbabwe is preparing a tougher legal response to counterfeit and substandard goods, a move that could reshape enforcement in tuckshops, informal markets and formal retail supply chains.</p>

<p>The Government is considering a dedicated Counterfeit Act after public complaints through consumer protection forums and awareness campaigns, according to Industry and Commerce Minister Mangaliso Ndlovu (<a href="https://iharare.com/zimbabwe-declares-war-on-fake-goods-with-tough-new-counterfeit-law/">iHarare</a>). The same proposal was reported by the Herald via Zimbabwe Situation, which said the planned law is meant to protect consumers, local industry and trade competitiveness (<a href="https://www.zimbabwesituation.com/news/govt-considers-tough-new-law-against-counterfeit-products/">Zimbabwe Situation</a>).</p>

<p>Ndlovu said counterfeit products are now hurting the national economy by reducing competitiveness, weakening revenue inflows to the fiscus and exposing consumers to health risks (<a href="https://www.zimbabwesituation.com/news/govt-considers-tough-new-law-against-counterfeit-products/">Zimbabwe Situation</a>). That matters because fake goods do not only undercut a shop shelf. They can also distort pricing, tax collection and trust in locally made products.</p>

<h2>Why the crackdown is moving beyond raids</h2>

<p>The enforcement numbers are already large. Authorities say more than 5,087 businesses have been inspected, over 560 prosecutions have been finalised, 589 compliance notices have been issued and 6,189 seizures have been effected (<a href="https://iharare.com/zimbabwe-declares-war-on-fake-goods-with-tough-new-counterfeit-law/">iHarare</a>). Those figures suggest Zimbabwe is not starting from zero, but officials argue the current system still needs a clearer legal tool.</p>

<h2>The health risk is the sharper edge</h2>

<p>The most direct warning came from earlier parliamentary evidence. Douglas Runyowa, the Ministry of Industry and Commerce's chief director for commerce, told legislators that a survey with the Standards Association of Zimbabwe found that 50% of sampled goods from informal retail shelves failed required standards (<a href="https://www.newzimbabwe.com/half-of-groceries-sold-by-tuckshops-vending-stalls-are-fake-unsafe-for-human-consumption-govt-finds/">NewZimbabwe</a>). ZimLive reported the same 50% finding and said the products were bought from shelves, tested, then compared against what was stated on the labels (<a href="https://www.zimlive.com/half-of-stock-sold-in-zimbabwean-tuck-shops-are-fake-goods-ministry-official/">ZimLive</a>).</p>

<p>Officials cited fake Vaseline, flour, rice and toothpaste among the problem products (<a href="https://www.zimlive.com/half-of-stock-sold-in-zimbabwean-tuck-shops-are-fake-goods-ministry-official/">ZimLive</a>). That is where the story becomes less abstract. A cheap fake toothpaste or bag of flour is not a clever bargain if nobody can verify what is inside it. That is easier said than done when incomes are tight.</p>

<h2>Local industry and AfCFTA are part of the same debate</h2>

<p>The counterfeit debate also sits inside Zimbabwe's regional trade ambitions. Zimbabwe is participating in the African Continental Free Trade Area, a market valued at about US$2.3 trillion according to the reports (<a href="https://www.zimbabwesituation.com/news/govt-considers-tough-new-law-against-counterfeit-products/">Zimbabwe Situation</a>). Buy Zimbabwe general manager Munyaradzi Hwengwere warned that Zimbabwe should use that market to grow, not become poorer through weak border and standards protection (<a href="https://iharare.com/zimbabwe-declares-war-on-fake-goods-with-tough-new-counterfeit-law/">iHarare</a>).</p>

<p>The new Consumer Protection Policy for 2026 to 2030 points in the same direction. It includes anti-counterfeiting enforcement targeted for 2027, stronger product safety systems and better cross-border cooperation (<a href="https://www.zimbabwesituation.com/news/consumer-protection-policy-to-boost-quality-fairness/">Zimbabwe Situation</a>). <p>The real test will be whether the planned Counterfeit Act can move beyond periodic raids and create predictable enforcement. If it does, formal manufacturers and consumers gain a clearer shield. If it does not, fake goods will remain another hidden tax on households already trying to stretch every dollar.</p>
